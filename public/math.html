1. The square‑root function

The parent square‑root function is $y=\sqrt{x}$. It is defined for $x\ge0$, and its outputs are non‑negative ($y\ge0$). Graphically it starts at the origin $(0,0)$ and increases slowly as $x$ grows.

Parent form

$y=\sqrt{x}$

Domain: $[0,\infty)$   Range: $[0,\infty)$

Key fact: It is the inverse of $y=x^2$ restricted to $x\ge0$.

Transformations

General transformed form:

y = a \sqrt{b(x-h)} + k
  • h: horizontal shift (right if $h>0$)
  • k: vertical shift (up if $k>0$)
  • a: vertical stretch / reflection when negative
  • b: horizontal stretch / compression (inside the root)
Quick example: $y=\sqrt{x-2}+3$ has domain $[2,\infty)$ and range $[3,\infty)$. The graph starts at $(2,3)$.

Worked example

Sketch and state domain/range of $y=2\sqrt{3(x+1)}-1$.

  1. Radicand: $3(x+1)\ge0 \Rightarrow x+1\ge0 \Rightarrow x\ge-1$  → domain $[-1,\infty)$.
  2. Start point: plug $x=-1$ → $y=2\sqrt{0}-1=-1$ → point $(-1,-1)$.
  3. Since $a=2>0$, graph rises to the right; range is $[-1,\infty)$.

2. The semicircle function

From the circle $ (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 $, solving for $y$ gives two choices: $y = k \pm \sqrt{r^2 - (x-h)^2}$. Choosing the $+$ sign gives the top semicircle (a function). Choosing $-$ gives the bottom semicircle (also a function).

Parent (top half)

$y = k + \sqrt{r^2 - (x-h)^2$}

Domain: $[h-r,\; h+r]$   Range (top): $[k,\; k+r]$

Shifts & radius

The semicircle is bounded horizontally by the circle's diameter; it is a finite, symmetric arc. If the center is $(h,k)$ and radius $r$ then:

  • Domain: $h-r \le x \le h+r$
  • Top semicircle range: $k \le y \le k+r$
  • Bottom semicircle range: $k-r \le y \le k$

Worked example

Let $y=\sqrt{25-x^2}$. This is the top semicircle with radius 5 centered at the origin.

Domain: $[-5,5]$, Range: $[0,5]$; top point at $(0,5)$ and endpoints $(-5,0)$ and $(5,0)$.

3. Comparison & key differences

  • Both use square roots: watch the radicand for domain restrictions.
  • Square‑root parent ($\sqrt{x}$) is unbounded to the right; semicircles are bounded (finite arc).
  • Semicircles come from solving a circle for $y$; choose a sign to keep single $y$ per $x$ (function).
